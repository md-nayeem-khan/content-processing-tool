\subsection{Introduction}\label{AcATJ_KrWLnZXH3Q58o61}
\\
\textbf{URL}(Uniform Resource Locator (URL), is an address that uniquely identifies a resource on the web.) \textbf{shortening} is a service that produces short aliases for long URLs, commonly referred to as \textbf{short links}. Upon clicking, these short links direct to the original URLs. The following illustration depicts how the process works:

\subsubsection{Advantages}\label{Qp7MhqRLBgZd-r-viwmDo}
\\
The key advantages of a URL shortening service are:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{BzXmEoewXmiuUj6dxC97_}
Shortened URLs are convenient to use: they optimize link usage across different forms of devices due to their enhanced accessibility and non-breakability.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{N5ckvVQBKpBnB3Jql85t9}
They are visually professional, engaging and facilitate a higher degree of sharing possibilities.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{WLJ3jrXBQ67pMzyIuwK-M}
They are less error-prone while typing.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{0Xs2n8TcBVzm6hCoVMix6}
They require smaller storage space at the user's end.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Disadvantages}\label{vKyduEge_YamkiCV7UkwX}
\\
The URL shortening service has some associated drawbacks as well. Some of them are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{HWbCL-367dHhT2B2V7JII}
We lose the originality of our brand by using a short URL generated by a third-party service. Many different brands using the same service get short URLs containing the same domain.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{7mahErh8WcRgn1zmL1emN}
Since we\textquotesingle re using a third-party service for URL shortening, the possibility of it getting shut down and wiping all our shortened URLs will always be there.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{5Q55hNJkqBCnLW518j67Y}
Our business brand image depends on the trustworthiness of a URL shortening service. The wrong business move might negatively impact our business. The competition for acquiring the in-demand custom short URLs is immense, so it might be possible that the best custom URLs are already taken by the time we start generating short URLs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Requirements for URL Shortening Design}\label{AGJuvJeZb_WVtn7aW83e9}
\\
Let's look at the functional and non-functional requirements for the service we'll be designing:

\subsubsection{Functional requirements}\label{vz4iMmiQt7BPdAo8QwXJA}

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{_FR4Nw6V2Mgptc0fiMgcp}
\textbf{Short URL generation}: Our service should be able to generate a unique shorter alias of the given URL.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{QyxlYu7__9jsQSmOAR_i6}
\textbf{Redirection}: Given a short link, our system should be able to redirect the user to the original URL.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{7DllASCXF5Oh8wI17UJ4Z}
\textbf{Custom short links:} Users should be able to generate custom short links for their URLs using our system.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{K11OBEkbsTkJV9ZDZjCrs}
\textbf{Deletion}: Users should be able to delete a short link generated by our system, given the rights.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{782nAJ-m8Ni1PI6hMHrGp}
\textbf{Update}: Users should be able to update the long URL associated with the short link, given the proper rights.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{qIeb5ms34YSL_k35OF8s9}
\textbf{Expiry time}: There must be a default expiration time for the short links, but users should be able to set the expiration time based on their requirements.
\end{itemize}
\\
\textbf{Note:~} Even though our system doesn't reuse expired short URLs, we still delete them after their five-year lifespan. Keeping them forever would cause the data store's search index to grow endlessly, increasing query time and adding unwanted latency.

\subsubsection{Non-functional requirements}\label{4JFdMyweyoPINPtn6GZtS}

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{rKtmODrY4hhos8_7IJ4aI}
\textbf{Availability}: Our system should be highly available, because even a fraction of a second of downtime would result in URL redirection failures. Since our system's domain is in URLs, we don't have the leverage of downtime, and our design must have fault-tolerance conditions instilled in it.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{LANXz3CIBx_T6QXrOeqE8}
\textbf{Scalability}: Our system should be horizontally scalable with increasing demand.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{6B-WKGmd1ZER4Li8Nibda}
\textbf{Readability}: The short links generated by our system should be easily readable, distinguishable, and typeable.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{WmdEETswVUM_KjLVmHcwi}
\textbf{Latency}: The system should perform at low latency to provide the user with a smooth experience.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{96ZdhLGSQ1vJ33CUgZr2y}
\textbf{Unpredictability}: From a security standpoint, the short links generated by our system should be highly unpredictable. This ensures that the next-in-line short URL is not serially produced, eliminating the possibility of someone guessing all the short URLs that our system has ever produced or will produce.
\end{itemize}
\\
\textbf{Note:}~Predictable short URLs can be a security risk; attackers might spot patterns and guess private links. To prevent this, our system avoids sequential IDs. Instead, it generates random, unpredictable identifiers, ensuring each short URL is unique, secure, and impossible to guess.

\subsubsection{Components}\label{QjdfyUgZy1pAlWPPNARRN}
\\
We'll explain the inner mechanism of different components within our system, as well as their usage as a part of the whole system, below. We 'll also highlight the design choices made for each component to achieve the overall functionality.
\\
\textbf{Database}: For services like URL shortening, there isn't a lot of data to store. However, the storage has to be horizontally scalable. The type of data we need to store includes:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{U9QSri_i_lasgnD4C3IZs}
User details.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{Q61gECHSh197CDSGqTydw}
Mappings of the URLs, that is, the long URLs that are mapped onto short URLs.
\end{itemize}
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Note:} For simplicity, let's assume that our service doesn't require user registration to generate a short URL, so we can skip adding certain data to our database.
\end{quote}
\\
Additionally, the stored records will have no relationships among themselves other than linking the URL-creating user's details, so we don't need structured storage for record-keeping. Considering the reasons above and the fact that our system will be read-heavy, NoSQL is a suitable choice for storing data. In particular, MongoDB is a good choice for the following reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{6zG4AtCvGJvGBxNEdT0Ga}
It uses a leader-follower protocol, making it possible to use replicas for heavy reading.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{O6vcMOVVOL1oAJyVXc-aq}
MongoDB ensures atomicity in concurrent write operations and avoids collisions by returning duplicate-key errors for record-duplication issues.
\end{enumerate}
\\
You're evaluating different NoSQL databases for a read-intensive service. Some options on the table are Cassandra, Riak, and MongoDB. Why might Cassandra or Riak not be good choices compared to MongoDB?

\textbf{Question:}

Why MongoDB outperforms Cassandra and Riak?

\textbf{Reference Answer:}

Cassandra and Riak are leaderless and optimized for high write throughput, which makes their reads slower due to read-repair and eventual consistency. Mongo DB's leader-based model offers faster, more consistent reads---making it better suited for read-intensive workloads.

\textbf{Short URL generator}: Our short URL generator will comprise a building block and an additional component:

\begin{itemize}
\item

A Base-58 encoder to enhance the readability of the short URL

To convert the numeric (base-10) IDs to alphanumeric (base-58), we'll need a base-10 for the base-58 encoder. We 'll explore the rationale behind these decisions alongside the internal working of the base-58 encoder in the upcoming sections.

\textbf{Other building blocks}: Beside the elements mentioned above, we'll also incorporate other building blocks like load balancers, cache, and rate limiters.

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{bAPmnSFamd2UwkbLNNDQu}
\textbf{Load balancing}: We can employ Global Server Load Balancing (GSLB) apart from local load balancing to improve availability. Since we have plenty of time between a short URL being generated and subsequently accessed, we can safely assume that our DB is geographically consistent and that distributing requests globally won't cause any issues.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{p3oA1XcUTEm2r6JqMAmFz}
\textbf{Cache}: For our specific read-intensive design problem, Memcached is the best choice for a cache solution. We require a simple, horizontally scalable cache system with minimal data structure requirements. Moreover, we'll have a data-center-specific caching layer to handle native requests. Having a global caching layer will result in higher latency.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{Q7kQ0LAQGvocj9pq4yEmA}
\textbf{Rate limiter}: Limiting each user's quota is preferable for adding a security layer to our system. We can achieve this by uniquely identifying users through their unique api\_dev\_key and applying one of the discussed rate-limiting algorithms (see \href{https://www.educative.io/courses/grokking-the-system-design-interview/system-design-the-rate-limiter}{Rate Limiter} from \emph{Building Blocks}). Keeping in view the simplicity of our system and the requirements, the fixed window counter algorithm would serve the purpose, as we can assign a set number of shortening and redirection operations per api\_dev\_key for a specific timeframe.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Managing unique ID allocation}\label{j3ObRWcrMWIjWyNmrneQ1}

On the backend, the server retrieves the base-10 equivalent of the generated short URL and marks it as unavailable in the ID range. This ensures that the same ID cannot be reassigned to another request. The diagram below shows how the system decodes a short URL and updates the database to track used and unused IDs.

\subsection{The scope of the short URL generator}\label{HnWWdZXrfhmIdEk4V2v33}
\\
The short URL generator is the backbone of our URL shortening service. The output of this short URL generator depends on the design-imposed limitations, as given below:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{SLM2AKFoxILOZ2CDrdUDv}
The generated short URL should contain alphanumeric characters.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{EILuv2_SOav_T2gyYd5N8}
None of the characters should look alike.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{0e8ltRrrJr1Jfv3l8xNO9}
The minimum default length of the generated short URL should be six characters.
\end{itemize}
\\
These limitations define the scope of our short URL generator. We can define the scope, as shown below:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{FJ-NvfVgX12OQxfwAmtc1}
\textbf{Starting range}: Our sequencer can generate a 64-bit binary number that ranges from \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_VEz18Prvv5fLfzFVJSWKJ}{{{\(1 \to (2^{64}-1)\)}}}. To meet the requirement for the minimum length of a short URL, we can select the sequencer IDs to start from at least 10 digits, i.e., 1 Billion.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{6ZVz4i1y0TgflehgXT9Pp}
\textbf{Ending point}: The maximum number of digits in sequencer IDs that map into the short URL generator\textquotesingle s output depends on the maximum utilization of 64 bits, that is, the largest base-10 number in 64-bits. We can estimate the total number of digits in any base by calculating these two points:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{L5VSSX8hP9mwHA8MYC9d4}
The numbers of bits to represent one digit in a base-n. This is given by \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_37rCZ7S8_p1uAQ-GkRmuA}{{{\(\text{log}_2{\text{n}}\)}}}.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{Ln-JC7gFtNtURGE9_WJ88}
\protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_dSEPWFDSyJ8ZzAXR7RCye}{{{\(\text{Number of digits} = \frac{\text{Total bits available}}{\text{Number of bits to represent one digit}}\)}}}
\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\\
Let\textquotesingle s see the calculations above for both the base-10 and base-58 mathematically:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{kqhs_nkDWh6kJ7DcKPGrM}
\textbf{Base-10:}

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{juWE_hyOGAFu5aOVoPAuP}
The number of bits needed to represent one decimal digit = \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_pCToVsgxH9fsAc48aggwd}{{{\(\text{log}_2{10}\)}}} = \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_-bml2CWk4BSPXmz9SuBwe}{{{\(3.13\)}}}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{j4ZleYqsWumCyt0O_CD2y}
The total number of decimal digits in 64-bit numeric ID = \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_tWNLkKl5zGyT5KJyKNf8s}{{{\(\frac{64}{3.13}\)}}} \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_30GTOlld0FozlHiNll9Gq}{{{\(=\)}}} \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_WME1epXBVeaXadATyKRwJ}{{{\(20\)}}}
\end{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{TkfXW7SCSSqIa_W32993A}
\textbf{Base-58:}

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{cDeSgzfUGDHt02wIl5xzk}
The number of bits needed to represent one decimal digit = \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_dc4Ed-y1SrkxjynfWTuIc}{{{\(\text{log}_2{5}8\)}}} \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_-OonFjRmjZrSRmE5cz3eu}{{{\(=\)}}} \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_Pv1pdmpqlf4tQJFXdHu-q}{{{\(5.85\)}}}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{ifdnwFYErKE6d-tG_JySU}
The total number of base-58 digits in a 64-bit numeric ID = \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_fWV3Zs0fVHKM642Lhb6PE}{{{\(\frac{64}{5.85}\)}}} \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_PPcYgPNeCR8bcj83tzazA}{{{\(=\)}}} \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_tl2XgC3xSHT3WULi6GDnC}{{{\(11\)}}}
\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Maximum digits}: The calculations above show that the maximum digits in the sequencer generated ID will be 20 and consequently, the maximum number of characters in the encoded short URL will be 11. 
\end{quote}

The sequencer's largest number, \(2^{64} - 1\) (that's 18,446,744,073,709,551,615), becomes \texttt{jpXCZedGfVQ} when converted to base-58; the longest possible short URL our system can generate!

Our system currently uses 10-digit and beyond sequencer IDs. What if we wanted to make them shorter? How could we use the sequencer ID range below 10 digits more effectively?

\textbf{Question:}

Using shorter sequencer IDs for custom short links

\textbf{Reference Answer:}

We can reserve sequencer IDs below 10 digits for premium users' custom short links. This allows us to:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
Reuse the lower ID range efficiently
\item
Offer shorter URLs (under six characters) For example, if a user requests \texttt{abc}, we assign it to their long URL and mark its decoded base-10 ID (e.g., 113019) as used, ensuring uniqueness.
\end{itemize}

\item
\begin{itemize}

Total numbers available in the sequencer = \protect\phantomsection\label{katex_inline_h6oymdExhzgX0tGsW6YDE}{{{\(2^{64} - 10^{9}\)}}} (starting from 1 Billion as discussed above)

\subsection{Requirements compliance}\label{DkMhDEdpTSzYdbs9ZWxLe}
\\
The last stage of a System Design is to evaluate it as per the non-functional requirements mentioned initially. Let's look at each metric one by one.

\subsubsection{Availability}\label{B59SvF9ESn1nWkqSsWeMw}
\\
We need high availability for users generating new short URLs and redirecting them based on the existing short URLs.
\\
Most of our building blocks, like databases, caches, and application servers have built-in replication that ensures availability and fault tolerance. The short URL generation system will not impact the availability either, as it depends on an easily replicable database of available and used unique IDs.
\\
To handle disasters, we can perform frequent backups of the storage and application servers, preferably twice a day, as we can't risk losing URLs data. We can use the Amazon S3 storage service for backups, as it facilitates cross-zonal replicating and restoration as well. In the worst-case scenario, we might lose 3.3 million (with 6.6 million daily requests assumed) newly generated short URLs that are not backed up on that specific day.
\\
Our design uses \textbf{global server load balancing (GSLB)} to handle our system traffic. It ensures intelligent request distribution among different global servers, especially in the case of on-site failures.
\\
We also apply a limit on the requests from clients to secure the intrinsic points of failures. To protect the system against DoS attacks, we use rate limiters between the client and web servers to limit each user's resource allocation. This will ensure a good and smooth traffic influx and mitigate the exploitation of system resources.

\subsubsection{Scalability}\label{FC3hRFkRHla4cvSKGWMmY}
\\
Our design is scalable because our data can easily be distributed among horizontally sharded databases. We can employ a consistent hashing scheme to balance the load between the application and database layers.

We can also use the range-based sharding while scaling our database server. However, if we partition our database based on a predefined range, it might lead to imbalanced partitions due to the variable distribution of URLs in each range. An example of this could be the distribution based on the first letter of the URL.

Our choice of the database for mapping URLs, MongoDB, also facilitates horizontal scaling. Some interesting reasons for selecting a NoSQL database are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{2zfh81SO33Wn3O6GHyE7d}
When a user accesses our system without logging in, our system doesn't save the \texttt{UserID}. Since we're flexible with storing data values, and it aligns more with the schematic flexibility provided by the NoSQL databases, using one for our design is preferable.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{2Qgk9d58zBXlIQYNz1NeV}
Scaling a traditional relational database horizontally is a daunting process and poses challenges to meeting our scalability requirements. We want to scale and automatically distribute our system's data across multiple servers. For this requirement, a NoSQL database would best serve our purpose.
\end{enumerate}
\\
Moreover, the large number of unique IDs available in the sequencer's design also ensures the scalability of our system.

\subsubsection{Readability}\label{zgMo7Jd5r33bqjhHRE-JO}
\\
The use of a base-58 encoder, instead of the base-64 encoder, enhances the readability of our system. We divide the readability into two sections:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{lY3_g_ZD6Xkd9E3OvhVeh}
\textbf{Distinguishable characters} like \texttt{0} (zero), \texttt{O} (capital o), \texttt{I} (capital i), and \texttt{l} (lower case L) are eliminated, excluding the possibility of mistaking a character for another look-alike character.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{mP2OvH35iR0Np307nyqhj}
\textbf{Non-alphanumeric characters} like \texttt{+} (plus) and \texttt{/} (slash) are also eliminated to only have alphanumeric characters in short URLs. Second, it also helps avoid other system-dependent encodings and makes the URLs readily available for all modern file systems and URLs schemes. Such characters may lead to undesired behavior and output during parsing.
\end{itemize}
\\
This non-functional requirement enhances the user interactivity of our system and makes short URL usage less error-prone for the users.

\subsubsection{Latency}\label{nmMIMPp46gB0fKAe_MWaX}
\\
Our system ensures low latency with its following features:

\begin{itemize}
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{9oT7nHnsitERO82LPEB-J}
Even the most time-consuming step across the short URL generation process, encoding, takes a few milliseconds. The overall time to generate a short URL is relatively low, ensuring there are no significant delays in this process.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{w6_FEhjybFkANZkEU9arO}
Our system is redirection-heavy. Writing on the database is minimal compared to reading, and its performance depends on how well it copes with all the redirection requests, compared to the shortening requests. We deliberately chose MongoDB because of its low latency and high throughput in reading-intensive tasks.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{nljf8ffrY6uz3sVyqEH3c}
Moreover, the probability of the user using the freshly generated short URL in the next few seconds is relatively low. During this time, synchronous replication to other locations is feasible and therefore adds to the overall low latency of the system for the user.
\item
\protect\phantomsection\label{W6PfM3fQeCIt4VQxjGe2i}
The deployment of a distributed cache in our design also ensures that the system redirects the user with the minimum possible delay.
\end{itemize}
\\
As a result of such design modifications, the system enjoys low latency and high throughput, providing good performance.

\subsubsection{Unpredictability}\label{sK0DAlaotl87WDtkDK0Ll}
\\
One of the requirements is to make our system's short URLs unpredictable, enhancing the security of our system.
\\
As the sequencer generates unique IDs in a sequence and distributes ranges among servers. Each server has a pre-assigned range of unique IDs, assigning them serially to the requests will make it easy to predict the following short URL. To counter it, we can randomly select a unique ID from the available ones and associate it to the long URL, encompassing the unpredictability of our system.
\\
